James' Home Depot Job

Saturday 5th July 2003

The Home Depot (pronounced de-poe) is the US equivalent of the UK's B&Q (although I think The Home Depot came first and actually owns B&Q).

Every couple of weeks the people at Home Depot invite children (yes children - are they mad!!!) to come in and make something.

This was James' second visit, and he invited along Jacob and Amaya.

The first time James went he made a "Gentleman's Box", which was supposed to be given to his lovely daddy for Fathers Day. James however decided to keep it himself for his special possessions (bits of string and stones).

This time they all had to make a planter box.

Each child is given a Home Depot apron, the same one employees wear. The are also given a "kit", comprising all the necessary parts and are let loose with glue, sanding blocks, hammers and nails. (I know - terrifying).

Children must be accompanied by an adult (OK so they are not that stupid) and build their kit on the premises.

An "Expert" (some old guy who made the kit last night), gives advice to the children (and adults/parents) on what to do, pitfalls to look out for etc.

Then the fun begins.

This usually (can say that as I have done this twice now) goes something like this.

  1. Dad (me) opens the kit.
  2. James hits the floor with the hammer.
  3. Dad removes hammer and gives James a sanding block.
  4. James throws sanding block and hammers floor.
  5. (Repeats for 5 minutes - details removed for brevity).
  6. Dad sands all pieces of wood (including the ones James has done, as they have just not been sanded properly).
  7. James looks for nails.
  8. Dad tries to explain to James how hammers work etc. (don't hit dads fingers, hit nail on the top, don't hit dads fingers, hit nail straight, don't hit dads fingers).
  9. Dad retrieves nails from hiding place.
  10. Dad assembles wood and starts the first nail off.
  11. James hits dads fingers, thumb, hand and wrist.
  12. Dad cries.
  13. Continue process until the item is assembled.
(In this particular assembly dad only had to pull the pieces apart once, due to nails sticking out the side.)

Total assembly time 20 minutes.

I should point out the place they give you to assemble the kit is like a sweatbox and I usually loose a few pounds dripping all over the floor (nice eh?)

Throughout the process, Abi amazingly sleeps soundly, despite the continuing hammering and shouting around us.

Once the planter box is finished each child gets to have it officially stamped (same old guy) and have their names written on it in black marker pen.

By now of course James is bored and wants something to eat.

All in all another great day out.

Question is, where am I going to hide the planter box?
